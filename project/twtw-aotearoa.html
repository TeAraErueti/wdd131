Te Whare Tapa Whā

Te Whare Tapa Whā is a holistic health model utilised by western society in Maori culture, New Zealand. The model itself was developed by Sir Mason Durie in 1984, which provides a framework for understanding the connections between health and well-being within Māori culture. The model represents health as a wharenui, also known as a meeting house. The meeting house is made up of four walls, each symbolizing a different aspect of well-being. The bottom of the wharenui is also crucial in which each wall is supported by the foundation of whenua, also known as the connection to the land. This approach emphasizes that all dimensions as a whole must be balanced in order to achieve overall health.

Taha wairua refers to spiritual health, encompassing one's connection to their heritage, beliefs, values, and traditions. This dimension emphasizes the importance of spirituality in overall wellbeing.

This dimension focuses on mental health and emotional stability. It involves understanding one's thoughts, feelings, and consciousness and how they influence overall wellbeing.

This dimension pertains to physical health and encompasses aspects such as nutrition, exercise, sleep hygiene, and general self-care practices. Maintaining taha tinana involves being active daily.

Taha whānau emphasizes the significance of family connections and social relationships in maintaining good health. It recognizes that strong familial bonds contribute positively to an individual's sense of identity and belonging.

At the core of Te Whare Tapa Whā lies the concept of whenua (land), which serves as the foundation upon which all four walls stand. This connection highlights the importance of environmental factors on individual health.

Māori Culture

Māori culture, known as Māoritanga, encompasses the customs, beliefs, and practices of the indigenous Māori people of Aotearoa (New Zealand). With roots tracing back over a thousand years to their Polynesian ancestors who arrived from Hawaiki, Māori culture is rich in tradition and spirituality. It plays a vital role in shaping New Zealand's national identity and continues to thrive amidst modern influences.
